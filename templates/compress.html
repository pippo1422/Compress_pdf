Complete Guide: How to Compress PDF Files Online

PDF files are the universal standard for document sharing, but they can grow surprisingly large — especially when packed with high-resolution images, embedded fonts, or complex vector graphics. Compressing PDFs reduces their file size so they're easier to email, upload, and store, all while preserving the information inside.

Our free online PDF compressor uses Ghostscript, the same industry-standard engine trusted by print houses, publishing companies, and IT departments worldwide. Ghostscript intelligently down-samples images, subsets fonts, removes duplicate objects, and optimizes the internal PDF structure — producing significantly smaller files without destroying readability.

Understanding Compression Levels

🟢 Low Compression (Prepress)

Preserves maximum quality at 300 DPI. Ideal when you need to print the PDF professionally. File size reduction is modest but quality is virtually untouched.

🟡 Medium Compression (Printer)

Maintains 300 DPI with optimized internal structures. Good for office printing and archiving. Noticeable size reduction with minimal visual difference.

🟠 High Compression (eBook)

Down-samples images to 150 DPI. The sweet spot for most users — great on-screen readability, substantial file size savings, perfect for email attachments.

🔴 Extreme Compression (Screen)

Reduces images to 72 DPI for the smallest possible file. Best for screen-only viewing, web uploads, and when size matters more than print quality.

When Should You Compress a PDF?

  • Email attachments: Most email providers cap attachments at 20–25 MB. Compression helps you stay under the limit.
  • Website uploads: Smaller PDFs load faster, improving user experience and SEO scores.
  • Cloud storage: Reduce storage costs by shrinking thousands of archived PDFs.
  • Mobile sharing: Compressed files download quicker on cellular networks, making document sharing on the go much smoother.
  • Form submissions: Government portals, university applications, and job sites often enforce strict file-size limits on uploaded documents.

Tips for Maximum Compression

  • Remove unnecessary pages before compressing — fewer pages means a smaller base file.
  • Use "Extreme" for drafts that only need to be read on screen and "Low" for documents headed to a professional printer.
  • Experiment with Custom DPI: A setting around 100–120 DPI often delivers a great balance between legibility and size.
  • Compress after merging: If you've combined multiple PDFs, run the merged file through the compressor to strip redundant data introduced during the merge.
  • Check the result: Always open the compressed PDF and verify images, charts, and text look acceptable before sharing.

Frequently Asked Questions About PDF Compression

How does Ghostscript compression work?

▼

Ghostscript rewrites the PDF from scratch, down-sampling images to the target DPI, subsetting fonts (keeping only the characters used), removing duplicate objects, and applying stream compression. The result is a standards-compliant PDF that is significantly smaller.

Will compression reduce the quality of my PDF?

▼

It depends on the level you choose. "Low" and "Medium" preserve nearly all visual quality. "High" reduces image resolution to 150 DPI — perfectly fine for on-screen reading. "Extreme" goes down to 72 DPI, which is noticeable on images but keeps text sharp. Text-heavy documents compress beautifully at every level.

What is the maximum file size I can upload?

▼

You can upload PDF files up to 100 MB. If your file is larger, consider splitting it into smaller parts first, compressing each, and then merging them back together.

Are my files stored on your servers?

▼

No. Uploaded files are deleted immediately after compression, and compressed files are removed after you download them (or automatically after one hour). We never store, share, or analyse your documents.

Can I compress password-protected PDFs?

▼

Encrypted or password-locked PDFs cannot be compressed until you remove the protection. Unlock the file first, compress it, and then reapply a password if needed.

What is DPI and how does it affect compression?

▼

DPI stands for "dots per inch." It controls the resolution of raster images inside the PDF. Higher DPI = sharper images but larger files. Lower DPI = smaller files but softer images. Text and vector graphics are unaffected by DPI changes.

Does compression change the page layout or text?

▼

No. Page dimensions, margins, text content, hyperlinks, and bookmarks remain identical. Only embedded raster images are down-sampled; everything else passes through untouched.
